# Finance Director — Agricultural Financial Reference

## Key Agricultural Financial Ratios & Benchmarks

### Liquidity
- **Current Ratio** (Current Assets / Current Liabilities): Strong ≥ 2.0, Caution 1.0–1.5, Critical < 1.0
- **Working Capital to Gross Revenue**: Target ≥ 25–35% for row crops; ≥ 15–20% for livestock

### Solvency
- **Debt-to-Asset Ratio** (Total Liabilities / Total Assets): Strong < 30%, Moderate 30–60%, Vulnerable > 60%
- **Equity-to-Asset Ratio**: Strong > 70%, Moderate 40–70%, Weak < 40%
- **Debt-to-Equity Ratio**: Healthy < 0.43, Caution 0.43–1.5, Critical > 1.5

### Profitability
- **Net Farm Income Ratio** (Net Farm Income / Gross Revenue): Strong > 25%, Average 10–25%, Weak < 10%
- **Operating Expense Ratio** (Operating Expenses / Gross Revenue): Efficient < 65%, Average 65–80%, High > 80%
- **Rate of Return on Assets (ROA)**: Target ≥ 5–8% for ag operations
- **Rate of Return on Equity (ROE)**: Target ≥ 8–12%

### Efficiency
- **Asset Turnover Ratio** (Gross Revenue / Total Assets): Row crops 0.25–0.45, Livestock 0.50–1.0, Specialty crops 0.40–0.70
- **Operating Profit Margin**: Row crops 15–25%, Dairy 8–15%, Beef cow-calf 10–20%, Poultry (contract) 5–12%
- **Interest Expense Ratio** (Interest / Gross Revenue): Target < 10%

### Repayment Capacity
- **Debt Service Coverage Ratio** (Net Income + Depreciation + Interest / Annual Debt Payments): Lender minimum 1.25, Strong ≥ 1.5
- **Term Debt Coverage Ratio**: Minimum 1.10–1.25 required by most lenders
- **Capital Replacement Margin**: Net income after family living, taxes, and debt payments — should be positive

## USDA/FSA Loan Programs

### Direct Operating Loans
- Max loan: $400,000
- Terms: 1–7 years (annual operating typically 12 months)
- Interest rate: set monthly, typically 1.5–3.0% below commercial rates
- Use: seed, fertilizer, livestock feed, equipment, family living expenses
- Requires: farm operating plan, 3 years financial history preferred

### Direct Farm Ownership Loans
- Max loan: $600,000
- Terms: up to 40 years
- Interest rate: fixed, set monthly by USDA
- Use: purchase farmland, construct/improve buildings, soil & water conservation
- Down payment: as low as 0% for direct loans
- Joint Financing: FSA guarantees up to 95% for commercial lenders

### Guaranteed Loans (via commercial lenders)
- Max loan: $2,029,000 (FY2024, adjusted annually for inflation)
- FSA guarantees 90% of loss on ownership, 95% on operating
- Borrower works with commercial bank; FSA provides guarantee
- Interest rate: negotiated with lender (typically prime + 1–2%)

### Emergency Loans
- Max loan: $500,000
- Triggered by Presidential or Secretarial disaster declaration
- Terms: 1–7 years operating, up to 40 years real estate
- Rate: typically lower than direct operating
- Must show inability to obtain credit elsewhere

### Microloans
- Max loan: $50,000
- Streamlined application (reduced paperwork)
- Terms: same as operating/ownership loans
- Designed for small, beginning, niche, and non-traditional operations
- No prior farm ownership experience required

### Beginning Farmer Provisions
- Up to 15 years farming experience qualifies
- Down payment program: 5% borrower equity required, FSA finances 45%, commercial lender 50%
- Reserved loan funds: FSA targets set-asides for beginning and socially disadvantaged farmers

## Farm Budgeting Frameworks

### Enterprise Budgets
- Revenue per unit (yield × price) for a single crop or livestock class
- Variable costs: seed, fertilizer, chemicals, fuel, labor, custom hire, vet/medicine
- Fixed costs: depreciation, insurance, interest on capital, property tax, land rent/mortgage
- Breakeven yield = Total costs / Expected price
- Breakeven price = Total costs / Expected yield
- Contribution margin = Revenue − Variable costs

### Whole-Farm Budget
- Aggregates all enterprise budgets plus overhead
- Includes family living withdrawals, non-farm income, capital purchases
- Key output: projected net farm income and change in net worth
- Should reconcile with balance sheet (beginning vs. ending net worth)

### Cash Flow Projections (Monthly/Quarterly)
- Map income timing: crop sales (harvest months), livestock sales, government payments, custom work
- Map expense timing: input purchases (spring), loan payments, property taxes, insurance premiums
- Identify peak borrowing needs (typically March–June for row crops)
- Operating line of credit sizing: peak negative cash balance + 10–15% buffer
- Include scheduled principal and interest payments separately

## Financial Metrics by Ag Business Type

### Row Crops (Corn, Soybeans, Wheat)
- Gross revenue per acre: $600–$1,200 depending on region and crop
- Operating cost per acre: $400–$750
- Land cost (rent): $150–$350/acre (Midwest), $50–$150 (Southern Plains)
- Breakeven corn price at 180 bu/acre: $3.50–$4.50/bu typical
- Target net income: $50–$200/acre after all costs

### Dairy
- Revenue per cwt: $18–$24 (Class III milk, volatile)
- Feed cost: 45–55% of total costs
- Breakeven: typically $16–$20/cwt all-in
- Capital intensity: $8,000–$15,000 invested per cow
- Income over feed cost (IOFC): target > $10/cwt

### Beef Cow-Calf
- Annual cow cost: $800–$1,200/head
- Calf revenue: $900–$1,500/head (550–650 lb calves)
- Breakeven calf price: $1.40–$2.00/lb
- Optimal stocking: 1.5–3.0 acres/cow (varies by region)
- Target return: $50–$200/cow net after all costs

### Poultry (Contract Broilers)
- Contract payment: $0.05–$0.08/lb live weight
- Grower investment per house: $250,000–$400,000
- Houses per farm: typically 4–8
- Annual gross per house: $50,000–$90,000
- Net margin after debt service: 5–12%

### Specialty Crops / Vegetables
- Gross revenue per acre: $5,000–$30,000+
- Labor cost: 30–50% of total expense
- Marketing cost: 10–20% (direct-to-consumer channels)
- Higher margins but higher volatility and perishability risk

## Cost Structures & Margin Benchmarks

### Typical Cost Breakdown (Row Crops)
- Seed: 10–15% of variable costs
- Fertilizer & lime: 25–35%
- Chemicals (herbicide/pesticide): 10–15%
- Fuel & repairs: 10–15%
- Crop insurance: 5–8%
- Labor: 5–10%
- Land (rent or ownership cost): 25–40% of total cost

### Margin Benchmarks by Sector
- Row crops: net margin 5–20% in good years, breakeven to negative in poor years
- Dairy: net margin 3–10%, highly cyclical
- Beef feedlot: net margin 2–8%, volume-dependent
- Hog finishing (contract): net margin 5–10%
- Specialty crops: net margin 15–35% with effective marketing
- Timber: low annual return (2–5%) but strong long-term asset appreciation

## Tax Considerations

### Section 179 Expensing
- 2024 limit: $1,220,000 (indexed for inflation)
- Phase-out begins at $3,050,000 in total equipment placed in service
- Applies to new and used tangible personal property (equipment, machinery, breeding livestock)
- Does not apply to real property (buildings, land improvements) unless qualified improvement property
- Must be placed in service during the tax year

### Bonus Depreciation
- 2024: 60% first-year bonus (declining 20% per year through 2027)
- 2025: 40%, 2026: 20%, 2027: 0% (absent legislative extension)
- Applies to new and used property with recovery period ≤ 20 years
- Grain bins, fencing, drainage tile qualify as 7- or 15-year property

### MACRS Depreciation Schedules (Common Ag Assets)
- 3-year: breeding hogs, racehorses (≥2 years old)
- 5-year: autos, light trucks, computers, cattle (dairy & breeding), breeding sheep/goats
- 7-year: farm machinery & equipment, grain bins, fencing, office furniture
- 10-year: single-purpose ag structures (fruit/nut trees, livestock confinement)
- 15-year: drainage tile, land improvements, roads, paved lots
- 20-year: general-purpose farm buildings

### Cash vs. Accrual Accounting
- Cash basis: income recognized when received, expenses when paid — used by ~85% of farms
- Advantages of cash: simplicity, defer income by delaying sales, prepay expenses for deduction
- Accrual: required if average gross receipts > $30 million (3-year average)
- Accrual advantage: more accurate profitability picture for management decisions
- Farmers can use special cash-method provisions even with inventories (farm exception)

### Other Key Tax Items
- Self-employment tax: 15.3% on net farm income (12.4% Social Security up to wage base + 2.9% Medicare)
- Farm income averaging: spread current-year income over prior 3 years to lower marginal rate
- Conservation Reserve Program (CRP): payments subject to SE tax if actively farming
- Like-kind exchange (1031): real property only (land for land); personal property excluded since 2018
- Installment sales: spread gain recognition over payment period for large land sales

## Working Capital Management for Seasonal Businesses

### Operating Line of Credit Sizing
- Calculate monthly cash flow projections for 12–18 months
- Peak borrowing typically March–June (planting) for row crops
- Size line at peak deficit + 15–20% cushion for price/yield variance
- Repay from harvest revenue (Oct–Dec) — lenders expect annual cleanup

### Cash Reserves
- Target 3–6 months of fixed expenses in liquid reserves
- Maintain separate operating account from savings/reserves
- Pre-harvest marketing (forward contracts) reduces cash flow uncertainty

### Accounts Receivable / Payable Management
- Negotiate early-pay discounts on inputs (2/10 net 30 = 36.7% annualized savings)
- Delay grain sales strategically for basis improvement or tax management
- Use warehouse receipts for short-term collateral borrowing

### Prepaid Expenses
- Fertilizer and chemical prepay programs: lock price and generate current-year deduction
- Limit prepaid deductions to 50% of other farm expenses (IRS prepay rule for cash-basis taxpayers)

## Capital Investment Analysis

### Net Present Value (NPV)
- Discount future net cash flows at cost of capital (typically 6–10% for ag)
- Accept project if NPV > 0
- Example: $200,000 tile drainage generating $30/acre on 200 acres over 20 years
  - Annual benefit: $6,000; at 7% discount rate, NPV ≈ $63,600 → accept

### Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
- Discount rate that makes NPV = 0
- Compare IRR to cost of capital (hurdle rate)
- Ag equipment IRR targets: 8–15% depending on risk
- Land IRR: 4–8% (lower threshold due to appreciation and collateral value)

### Payback Period
- Time to recover initial investment from net cash flows
- Simple payback: ignores time value of money
- Typical targets: equipment 3–7 years, buildings 7–15 years, land 15–25+ years
- Lenders often want payback within useful life of asset

### Key Benchmarks for Common Ag Investments
- Center pivot irrigation ($100K–$180K): payback 4–8 years, IRR 10–18%
- Grain storage (on-farm bin): payback 5–10 years, captures $0.15–$0.40/bu basis improvement
- Tile drainage: payback 6–12 years, yield increase 10–25% on poorly drained soils
- Precision ag technology (GPS, VRT): payback 2–5 years, saves 5–15% on inputs
- Land purchase: long payback but 3–5% annual appreciation historically (Midwest cropland)

### Lease vs. Buy Analysis
- Compare after-tax cost of leasing vs. owning (include tax benefits of depreciation)
- Operating lease: lower commitment, flexible, 100% deductible; no ownership equity
- Ownership: builds equity, depreciation benefits, higher upfront cost
- Break-even utilization: if annual use < 60–70% of capacity, leasing often preferred
- Custom hire alternative: no capital commitment, pay per acre/hour, best for occasional needs
