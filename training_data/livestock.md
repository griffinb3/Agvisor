# Livestock & Animal Systems — Reference Knowledge Base

## Beef Cattle Management

### Cow-Calf Operations
- Typical cow-calf cycle: calving season (spring or fall), breeding season 60–90 days, weaning at 6–8 months
- Target calving interval: 365 days; cows that fail to breed within 80–85 days post-calving should be evaluated
- Body condition score (BCS) at calving should be 5–6 (on 1–9 scale) to ensure adequate rebreeding rates
- First-calf heifers require BCS of 6 at calving; breed heifers 30 days ahead of mature cows
- Replacement heifer target: reach 60–65% of mature body weight by breeding at 14–15 months of age
- Annual cow cost typically ranges $800–$1,200 depending on region and feed costs

### Calving Management
- Monitor heifers every 2–3 hours during calving; mature cows every 4–6 hours
- Stage 1 labor (cervix dilation): 2–6 hours; Stage 2 (delivery): 30 min to 2 hours
- Intervene if no progress after 1 hour in heifers, 2 hours in mature cows during Stage 2
- Ensure calves receive 1–2 quarts of quality colostrum within 2 hours of birth; total 10% of body weight in first 24 hours
- Navel dip with 7% iodine solution immediately after birth

### Stocker Operations
- Target average daily gain (ADG): 1.5–2.5 lbs/day on grass; 2.5–3.5 lbs/day with supplementation
- Receiving protocol: process on arrival (vaccinate, deworm, implant), provide low-stress handling, free-choice hay and water
- Morbidity targets: <15% during receiving; death loss <1.5%

### Feedlot Operations
- Typical finishing period: 120–180 days; target ADG 3.0–4.0 lbs/day
- Feed conversion ratio (FCR) benchmark: 5.5–6.5 lbs feed per lb gain
- Target hot carcass weight: 800–950 lbs; yield grade 2–3; quality grade Choice or better
- Cost of gain is the primary driver of profitability; track closely against market price

## Dairy Management

### Milking Systems
- Parlor types: herringbone, parallel, rotary, robotic (AMS)
- Robotic milking systems average 2.8–3.2 milkings/cow/day; parlor systems typically 2x/day
- Milking routine: pre-dip, strip and forestrip check, attach units, post-dip; total unit-on time 4–7 minutes
- Vacuum levels: 10–13 inches Hg for most systems; pulsation rate 55–65 cycles/min

### DHI Records and Milk Quality
- Dairy Herd Improvement (DHI) testing: monthly milk weights, components, somatic cell count (SCC)
- Target bulk tank SCC: <200,000 cells/mL; individual cow goal <200,000
- SCC penalties typically begin at 400,000 cells/mL; premium payments often below 150,000
- Target milk components: butterfat 3.5–4.0%, protein 3.0–3.3%, solids-not-fat >8.5%
- Rolling herd average (RHA) benchmark for Holstein herds: 22,000–28,000 lbs/cow/year

### Dairy Nutrition
- Fresh cow rations: high-quality forage, 16–18% CP, adequate fiber (>28% NDF)
- Peak milk response: 1 lb additional milk for every 0.5 lb additional DMI above maintenance
- Transition period (3 weeks pre- to 3 weeks post-calving) is the most critical nutritional phase

## Swine Production

### Farrow-to-Finish Operations
- Gestation length: 114 days (3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days)
- Target litter size: 12–14 pigs born alive; pre-weaning mortality goal <10%
- Weaning age: typically 19–24 days; weaning weight target 12–15 lbs
- Pigs/sow/year (PSY) benchmark: 25–30 pigs weaned per sow per year
- Sow replacement rate: 40–50% annually

### Nursery Management
- Nursery phase: weaning to ~50 lbs (approximately 6–8 weeks)
- Target nursery ADG: 0.9–1.1 lbs/day; FCR 1.4–1.7
- Temperature at placement: 85°F, reduce 2°F per week
- Mortality target: <2.5% in nursery phase

### Grow-Finish
- Grow-finish phase: 50 lbs to market weight (270–290 lbs)
- Target ADG: 1.8–2.2 lbs/day; FCR 2.6–3.0
- Days to market from birth: approximately 165–185 days
- Mortality target: <3% in grow-finish phase
- Feed cost represents 60–70% of total production cost

## Poultry Production

### Broiler Management
- Market age: 35–56 days depending on target weight (4.5–9.0 lbs live weight)
- FCR benchmark: 1.6–1.9 (lbs feed per lb live weight)
- Target mortality: <4% for the flock
- Stocking density: 0.75–1.0 sq ft per bird; optimal temperature 70–75°F after brooding
- Brooding temperature: 90–95°F at placement, reduce 5°F per week

### Layer Management
- Pullet phase: 0–18 weeks; hens begin laying at 18–22 weeks
- Peak production: 90–95% hen-day production at 26–30 weeks of age
- Feed consumption: 0.22–0.26 lbs/hen/day; target FCR 3.5–4.0 lbs feed per dozen eggs
- Egg weight increases with hen age; shell quality decreases
- Lighting program: 14–16 hours of light per day during production

### Turkey Management
- Market age: hens 14–16 weeks (15–20 lbs); toms 18–22 weeks (35–45 lbs)
- FCR: 2.2–2.8 depending on market weight
- Mortality target: <6% for the flock

## Animal Nutrition Fundamentals

### Energy
- Energy systems: TDN (total digestible nutrients), NEm/NEg (net energy for maintenance/gain), ME (metabolizable energy)
- Mature beef cow maintenance requirement: approximately 10–12 Mcal NEm/day (1,200 lb cow)
- Energy is the most common nutritional deficiency in grazing livestock

### Protein
- Crude protein (CP) = nitrogen x 6.25; does not distinguish between true protein and NPN
- Ruminant protein systems: rumen degradable protein (RDP) and rumen undegradable protein (RUP)
- Beef cow CP requirements: 7–12% of diet DM depending on stage of production
- Dairy cow CP requirements: 15–18% of diet DM depending on milk production level
- Swine requirements: lysine is the first limiting amino acid; formulate on digestible amino acid basis

### Minerals and Vitamins
- Macro minerals: calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, potassium, sodium, chloride, sulfur
- Ca:P ratio should be maintained between 1.5:1 and 2:1 for most livestock species
- Trace minerals of concern: copper, zinc, selenium, manganese, cobalt, iodine
- Selenium supplementation critical in deficient areas; FDA limit 0.3 ppm in complete feed
- Vitamin A is the most commonly deficient vitamin in cattle on dormant forage

### Feed Analysis Interpretation
- Standard analysis: DM, CP, ADF, NDF, TDN, minerals
- NDF (neutral detergent fiber): measures total cell wall; inversely related to intake in forages
- ADF (acid detergent fiber): correlates with digestibility; lower ADF = higher digestibility
- Relative Feed Value (RFV): 100 = full-bloom alfalfa; >150 = premium quality
- Relative Forage Quality (RFQ): more accurate than RFV; accounts for fiber digestibility

## Feed Formulation Basics

### Least-Cost Rations
- Formulate to meet nutrient requirements at minimum cost using available feedstuffs
- Use Pearson Square for simple two-feed ration balancing
- Linear programming software (e.g., AMTS, CPM Dairy, NDS) for complex ration formulation
- Always verify rations meet minimum fiber requirements to prevent acidosis in ruminants

### Forage Quality
- Harvest timing is the most important factor affecting forage quality
- Alfalfa: harvest at late bud to 10% bloom; grass hay: harvest at boot to early head stage
- Corn silage: harvest at 65% whole-plant moisture (32–35% DM)
- Baleage/wrapped silage: target 45–55% moisture; critical to achieve anaerobic conditions

### Feed Additives
- Ionophores (monensin, lasalocid): improve feed efficiency 5–10% in cattle; also aid coccidiosis prevention
- Beta-agonists (ractopamine): improve feed efficiency and lean gain in finishing cattle and swine
- Probiotics/direct-fed microbials: support rumen function during stress periods
- Buffers (sodium bicarbonate): used in high-grain dairy rations to maintain rumen pH above 6.0

## Breeding and Genetics

### Expected Progeny Differences (EPDs)
- EPDs predict the average performance of an animal's offspring relative to breed average
- Key beef EPDs: birth weight, weaning weight, yearling weight, milk, marbling, ribeye area
- Use EPD percentile rankings rather than raw values for cross-breed comparisons
- Accuracy values indicate reliability; higher accuracy = more progeny data

### Genomic Testing
- DNA tests (e.g., GeneSeek, Zoetis) can improve EPD accuracy on young animals
- Parentage verification, genetic defect screening, and tenderness markers available
- Genomic-enhanced EPDs (GE-EPDs) combine DNA markers with traditional performance data

### Crossbreeding Systems
- Heterosis (hybrid vigor): 15–25% improvement in lifetime productivity in beef cattle
- Terminal crossbreeding: all calves marketed; maximizes individual heterosis
- Rotational crossbreeding (2- or 3-breed): maintains maternal heterosis in replacements
- Composite breeds: stabilized crossbred populations; moderate heterosis retained

### Artificial Insemination (AI) Programs
- Estrus synchronization protocols: 7-day CO-Synch + CIDR, 14-day CIDR-PG
- Timed AI (TAI) conception rates: 50–65% in well-managed programs
- Fixed-time AI eliminates heat detection; increases AI usage in beef herds
- Semen handling: maintain liquid nitrogen tanks; thaw straws at 95°F for 45 seconds

## Common Diseases and Prevention

### Bovine Viral Diarrhea (BVD)
- Persistently infected (PI) calves are the primary reservoir; test and remove PIs from herd
- Vaccination: modified-live virus (MLV) vaccines for open heifers and cows pre-breeding
- Economic impact: reproductive losses, immunosuppression, increased susceptibility to BRD

### Bovine Respiratory Disease (BRD)
- Leading cause of death in feedlot cattle; costs U.S. industry >$900 million/year
- Pathogens: Mannheimia haemolytica, Pasteurella multocida, Histophilus somni, Mycoplasma bovis
- Risk factors: stress, commingling, shipping, weather changes, inadequate vaccination
- Prevention: preconditioning programs, low-stress handling, modified-live vaccines pre-shipping
- Metaphylaxis: mass antibiotic treatment at arrival for high-risk cattle

### Calf Scours
- Leading cause of death in calves <30 days of age
- Common agents: E. coli (1–3 days), rotavirus/coronavirus (5–14 days), Cryptosporidium (5–21 days), coccidia (>21 days)
- Prevention: colostrum management, clean calving environment, Scour Guard-type vaccines to dams pre-calving
- Treatment: oral electrolytes for mild cases; IV fluids and antibiotics for severe cases

### Mastitis
- Clinical mastitis: visible milk changes, swelling; subclinical: elevated SCC only
- Major pathogens: Staph. aureus, Strep. agalactiae (contagious); E. coli, Strep. uberis (environmental)
- Prevention: proper milking hygiene, post-milking teat dipping, dry cow therapy, culling chronic cows
- National Mastitis Council (NMC) 10-point mastitis control plan as industry standard

### Vaccination Protocols
- Beef calves: 7-way clostridial + respiratory (IBR, BVD, BRSV, PI3) at branding/turnout; booster at weaning
- Dairy calves: intranasal respiratory at birth; clostridial and systemic respiratory at 2–4 months
- Swine: PCV2 (circovirus), Mycoplasma hyopneumoniae at 3–4 weeks; PRRSv per herd status
- Poultry: Marek's disease at hatch; Newcastle disease and infectious bronchitis per schedule

## Pasture Management

### Rotational Grazing
- Divide pastures into paddocks; move livestock based on forage height, not calendar
- Start grazing: 8–10 inches for cool-season grasses; stop grazing at 3–4 inch residual
- Rest periods: 21–45 days depending on forage species, season, and growth rate
- Intensive rotational grazing can increase carrying capacity 25–40% over continuous grazing

### Stocking Rates
- Expressed as animal units (AU) per acre; 1 AU = 1,000-lb cow with calf
- Typical stocking rate: 1.0–2.5 AU/acre for improved pasture; 10–50 acres/AU for rangeland
- Match stocking rate to forage production; overgrazing reduces stand persistence and soil health
- Adjust stocking rate seasonally or use sacrifice paddocks during drought

### Forage Species Selection
- Cool-season grasses: tall fescue, orchardgrass, bromegrass, perennial ryegrass, Kentucky bluegrass
- Warm-season grasses: bermudagrass, bahiagrass, big bluestem, switchgrass, eastern gamagrass
- Legumes: white clover, red clover, alfalfa, lespedeza — fix nitrogen, improve forage quality
- Toxic fescue (endophyte-infected): causes fescue toxicosis; use novel endophyte or endophyte-free varieties
- Stockpiled tall fescue: excellent fall/winter grazing in the transition zone

## Production Benchmarks by Species

### Beef Cattle
- Average weaning weight: 550–650 lbs at 205 days adjusted
- Cow conception rate target: >90% in 60-day breeding season
- Calf death loss (birth to weaning): <3%
- Breakeven cow cost vs. calf value is the key profitability metric

### Dairy Cattle
- Average U.S. milk production: ~24,000 lbs/cow/year (Holstein average)
- Calving interval target: 12.5–13.5 months
- Conception rate: 35–45% for first service; overall pregnancy rate 20–25% per 21-day cycle
- Cull rate: 30–38% annually; involuntary culling should be <15%

### Swine
- Pigs weaned/sow/year: 25–30
- Wean-to-finish mortality: <5%
- Wean-to-finish FCR: 2.5–2.8 (top 25% of producers)
- Full-value pigs per sow per year: target >24

### Poultry
- Broiler liveability: >96%
- Broiler FCR: 1.6–1.85 at 6 lbs live weight
- Layer hen-day production: 85–90% averaged over full cycle (18–72 weeks)
- Layer feed conversion: 3.5–4.0 lbs feed per dozen eggs

## Animal Welfare Standards and Auditing

### General Principles
- Five Freedoms: freedom from hunger/thirst, discomfort, pain/injury/disease, fear/distress, and freedom to express normal behavior
- Welfare audits assess: body condition, lameness, handling practices, housing, water/feed access, euthanasia protocols
- Third-party audit programs: BQA (Beef Quality Assurance), FARM (dairy), PQA Plus (swine), NCC (poultry)

### Key Audit Areas
- Handling and transport: low-stress methods, electric prod use <5% of animals, non-slip flooring
- Euthanasia: written protocols required; approved methods by species (captive bolt, gunshot, injectable agents)
- Housing: adequate space allowances per species guidelines; proper ventilation and temperature management
- Pain management: dehorning/castration protocols should include analgesics; trend toward earlier age procedures
- Record keeping: mortality records, treatment records, training documentation for all animal handlers
- Antibiotic stewardship: Veterinary Feed Directive (VFD) compliance; judicious use principles; no growth promotion with medically important antibiotics since 2017
