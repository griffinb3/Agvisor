# Agronomist Knowledge Base

## Major Row Crop Production Guides

### Corn
- Planting dates: Mid-April to late May depending on region; soil temp at 50°F minimum at 2-inch depth
- Seeding rates: 30,000–36,000 seeds/acre; adjust based on yield environment and hybrid
- Row spacing: 30-inch standard; 20-inch rows can boost yield 3–5% in northern geographies
- Fertility needs: 1.0–1.2 lb N per bushel yield goal; 0.4 lb P2O5 per bushel removal; 0.27 lb K2O per bushel removal
- GDDs to maturity: ~2,700 GDD (base 50°F) for a 111-day hybrid
- Critical growth stages: V6 (ear row determination), V12–VT (ear size/kernel set), R1 silking (pollination)

### Soybeans
- Planting dates: Late April to early June; optimal before May 20 in Corn Belt
- Seeding rates: 120,000–140,000 seeds/acre; target final stand of 100,000–120,000
- Row spacing: 15-inch or drilled improves canopy closure and yield vs 30-inch
- Fertility needs: Fix 50–60% of N via Bradyrhizobium inoculation; 0.8 lb P2O5 per bushel removal; 1.4 lb K2O per bushel removal
- Critical growth stages: R1 (flowering), R3 (pod set), R5–R6 (seed fill)

### Wheat (Winter)
- Planting dates: September 15 – October 15 in central Corn Belt; after Hessian fly-free date
- Seeding rates: 1.2–1.6 million seeds/acre; adjust for late planting (increase 10–15%)
- Fertility needs: 1.2–1.5 lb N per bushel yield goal; split-apply N in fall and spring topdress at greenup
- Critical growth stages: Feekes 5 (jointing), Feekes 10.5 (flowering — key fungicide timing)

## Soil Health Principles

- Soil organic matter (SOM): Target >3% for productive cropland; each 1% SOM holds ~20,000 gal water/acre
- Aggregate stability: Promotes water infiltration, reduces erosion; improved by reduced tillage and cover crops
- Water infiltration: Healthy soils infiltrate >1 inch/hour; compaction and surface crusting reduce rates dramatically
- Biological activity: Earthworm counts >10 per shovelful indicate good biology; mycorrhizal networks aid P uptake
- Five principles of soil health: minimize disturbance, keep soil covered, maintain living roots, diversify species, integrate livestock when possible
- Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio: Ideal soil C:N is ~10:1; high-C residues (corn stover, cereal straw) tie up N during decomposition

## Soil Testing Interpretation

- pH: Corn optimal 6.0–6.5; soybeans 6.0–7.0; wheat 6.0–7.0; lime if below 6.0 using buffer pH to determine rate
- Buffer pH (SMP or Sikora): Indicates lime requirement; lower buffer pH = more lime needed; apply 2–4 tons ENM/acre when buffer pH <6.4
- Phosphorus (Bray-1 or Mehlich-3): Optimum 20–30 ppm; build applications at 10 lb P2O5/acre per ppm below optimum
- Potassium: Optimum 150–200 ppm; sandy soils lose K more readily; apply maintenance + buildup as needed
- Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): Sandy soils 5–10 meq/100g; silt loams 15–25; clays 25–40+; higher CEC = greater nutrient-holding capacity
- Micronutrients: Zinc critical for corn at <1.5 ppm; manganese deficiency common in high-pH soils (>7.3); sulfur increasingly deficient in many regions at <10 ppm
- Organic matter: Tested as % by weight; each 0.1% increase represents ~2,000 lb/acre of organic material

## Nutrient Management

### Nitrogen
- Corn: 150–220 lb N/acre depending on yield goal and soil type; credit 40–50 lb N/acre following soybeans
- Split applications: 30–50% at planting (starter or pre-plant), remainder sidedressed at V6–V8
- Enhanced efficiency fertilizers: Polymer-coated urea, nitrification inhibitors (N-Serve, Instinct) reduce losses
- Loss pathways: Denitrification in saturated soils, leaching on sandy soils, volatilization from surface-applied urea

### Phosphorus
- Starter fertilizer: 2x2 placement of 5–10 gal 10-34-0/acre benefits early vigor in cool soils
- Broadcast and incorporate: For buildup applications; band placement 10–15% more efficient than broadcast
- Stratification risk: No-till fields accumulate P in top 2 inches; deep banding can correct

### Potassium
- Corn removal: ~0.27 lb K2O per bushel; soybeans remove ~1.4 lb K2O per bushel (heavy removal crop)
- Application timing: Fall-apply on medium-to-heavy soils; spring-apply on sandy soils to reduce leaching
- Tissue testing: Ear-leaf K at silking should be >1.7% for corn

## Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

- Scouting: Walk fields weekly during growing season; use W-pattern or zigzag sampling; check 5+ locations per field
- Economic thresholds: Treat only when pest populations exceed economic injury levels
  - Corn rootworm beetles: 0.75 beetles/plant at silking if corn-on-corn
  - Soybean aphids: 250 aphids/plant with increasing populations and plants in R1–R5
  - Western bean cutworm: 5% of corn plants with egg masses
- Biological controls: Lady beetles and lacewings for aphids; Bt traits for lepidopteran pests; Trichoderma for root disease suppression
- Cultural controls: Crop rotation breaks pest cycles; delayed planting avoids early-season insect pressure; residue management reduces overwintering sites
- Resistance management: Rotate insecticide modes of action (MOA); use refuge areas for Bt crops per EPA guidelines

## Common Crop Diseases

### Corn
- Gray leaf spot (Cercospora zeae-maydis): Rectangular lesions; worst in continuous corn with heavy residue; fungicide at VT-R1 if susceptible hybrid
- Northern corn leaf blight (Exserohilum turcicum): Cigar-shaped lesions; yield loss if infection before R2; Ht genes provide partial resistance
- Tar spot (Phyllachora maydis): Black raised stromata on leaves; increasing threat in upper Midwest; fungicide at VT effective when applied preventively

### Soybeans
- Sudden death syndrome (SDS — Fusarium virguliforme): Root rot + foliar symptoms at R3–R6; manage with resistant varieties, improved drainage, avoid early planting in cold soils
- White mold (Sclerotinia sclerotiorum): Favored by cool, wet canopies; wider rows, lower seeding rates reduce humidity; fungicide (Endura) at R1–R3
- Soybean cyst nematode (SCN): #1 yield robber; soil-sample to detect; rotate resistant varieties (PI 88788 vs Peking sources); egg counts >2,000/100cc soil warrant action

## Weed Management

- Pre-emerge herbicides: Apply within 3 days of planting for residual control; require 0.5-inch rainfall for activation
  - Corn: atrazine + metolachlor (Groups 5+15), acetochlor (Group 15), mesotrione (Group 27)
  - Soybeans: sulfentrazone + metribuzin (Groups 14+5), flumioxazin (Group 14), pyroxasulfone (Group 15)
- Post-emerge herbicides: Apply when weeds are 2–4 inches tall for best efficacy
  - Glyphosate (Group 9): Broad spectrum but resistance widespread in waterhemp, Palmer amaranth, marestail
  - Glufosinate (Group 10): Contact herbicide, requires good coverage; works on glyphosate-resistant species
  - Dicamba (Group 4): Effective on broadleaves; use low-volatility formulations; follow label buffer distances
- Resistance management: Use at least 2 effective sites of action in every application; rotate chemistries annually; do not allow escapes to go to seed
- Herbicide-resistant weeds of concern: Waterhemp (multiple resistance), Palmer amaranth (ALS+glyphosate+PPO), giant ragweed, kochia

## Precision Agriculture Tools

- Soil sampling: 2.5-acre grid sampling or zone-based sampling using EC/topography maps; sample 0–8 inch depth every 4 years
- Variable rate seeding: Increase population on high-yield zones, decrease on stress-prone areas; typical range 28,000–36,000 seeds/acre for corn
- Variable rate fertilizer: Apply P and K based on grid or zone soil test results; build low-testing areas, maintain optimum areas
- NDVI imagery: Assess crop vigor mid-season; use satellite or drone platforms; identifies stress zones for targeted scouting
- Yield data analysis: Clean data (remove header/end rows, outliers); normalize across years; identify consistent low-yielding zones for investigation
- As-applied maps: Verify actual vs intended application rates; diagnose equipment or calibration issues
- RTK guidance: Sub-inch accuracy for planting, spraying, and harvesting; reduces overlap and skips

## Cover Crop Selection Guide

### Cereal Rye
- Benefits: Excellent biomass producer (2–4 tons DM/acre), suppresses weeds, scavenges residual N, prevents erosion
- Planting window: September–November; can be aerially seeded into standing crops
- Seeding rate: 50–70 lb/acre drilled; 70–90 lb/acre broadcast
- Termination: Burndown with glyphosate or roller-crimper at anthesis; terminate 10–14 days before cash crop planting

### Crimson Clover
- Benefits: Fixes 60–120 lb N/acre; attracts pollinators; good biomass in mild climates
- Planting window: August–October in transition zone; not winter-hardy north of Zone 6
- Seeding rate: 15–20 lb/acre drilled
- Termination: Winter-kills in cold climates; herbicide or mowing in spring where it survives

### Tillage Radish (Daikon)
- Benefits: Breaks compaction layers (taproot penetrates 12–30 inches); scavenges N from depth; decomposes rapidly
- Planting window: August–September; needs 60 days of growth before hard freeze
- Seeding rate: 8–10 lb/acre drilled
- Termination: Winter-kills at ~20°F; leaves channels for water infiltration in spring

## Crop Rotation Benefits and Planning

- Corn–soybean rotation: Yields 10–15% more corn than continuous corn; soybean N credit of 40–50 lb/acre
- Extended rotations: Corn–soybean–wheat/cover crop reduces disease pressure, improves soil health, spreads labor demands
- Rotation breaks pest cycles: Corn rootworm, SCN, soybean diseases all managed through rotation
- Economic considerations: Balance crop prices, input costs, and agronomic benefits; rotations stabilize long-term profitability
- Diversity benefits: Different root architectures access different soil zones; varied residue types improve soil biology

## Yield-Limiting Factors Diagnosis

- Compaction: Check with penetrometer (>300 psi = restrictive); diagnose by root restriction at plow layer (6–10 inches)
- Drainage: Ponding >24 hours causes root damage; tile drainage at 30–40 ft spacing in poorly drained soils
- Nutrient deficiency: V-shaped yellowing from leaf tip = N deficiency in corn; interveinal chlorosis = Mn or Fe deficiency
- Plant population: Harvest population below 28,000/acre in corn suggests emergence or stand issues
- Weather stress: Drought at pollination can reduce corn yield 5–8% per day of stress; excessive heat (>95°F) during flowering reduces pollen viability
- Soil pH: Yield loss begins when pH drops below 5.5; aluminum toxicity restricts roots at low pH

## Irrigation Management

- Soil moisture monitoring: Use tensiometers, capacitance probes, or neutron probes; target 50–60% plant-available water depletion before irrigating
- ET-based scheduling: Calculate crop water use from reference ET × crop coefficient (Kc); corn peak ET = 0.30–0.35 inches/day
- Pivot management: Apply 0.75–1.0 inch per pass; avoid runoff by matching application rate to soil intake rate
- Critical irrigation periods: Corn VT–R3 (2.5–3.0 inches/week demand); soybeans R3–R5 (1.5–2.0 inches/week)
- Water use efficiency: Deficit irrigation strategies save 20–30% water with <10% yield loss in some environments
- Chemigation: Apply fertilizer or crop protection through pivot; requires anti-siphon valves and check valves per state regulations
- Scheduling tools: Use apps (CornSoyWater, WaterMark sensors) to optimize timing and amount; avoid both under- and over-irrigation
