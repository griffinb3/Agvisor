# Risk Management & Insurance Reference Guide for Agricultural Operations

## Federal Crop Insurance Programs

### Multi-Peril Crop Insurance (MPCI)
- Federally subsidized insurance administered through USDA Risk Management Agency (RMA)
- Sold and serviced by approved private insurance companies (AIPs)
- Covers losses from natural causes: drought, excess moisture, hail, wind, frost, disease, insects
- Available for over 100 crops; coverage and terms vary by county and crop
- Sales closing dates vary by crop and region — typically well before planting
- Producers must insure all acreage of an insured crop in the county (no cherry-picking fields)

### Revenue Protection (RP)
- Most popular federal crop insurance plan — accounts for ~70% of policies
- Guarantees a revenue level based on APH yield × projected price
- Projected price set during discovery period using futures (e.g., CBOT December corn futures in February)
- Harvest price determined during harvest-period futures window
- Revenue guarantee increases if harvest price exceeds projected price (upside price protection)
- Indemnity triggered when actual revenue (yield × harvest price) falls below guaranteed revenue
- Available coverage levels: 50%, 55%, 60%, 65%, 70%, 75%, 80%, 85%
- Enterprise units reduce premiums by pooling all acres of a crop in a county
- Optional units allow field-by-field or section-by-section coverage at higher premium

### Revenue Protection with Harvest Price Exclusion (RP-HPE)
- Same as RP but the guarantee does not increase with rising harvest prices
- Lower premium than standard RP
- Suitable when producer has already locked in prices via forward contracts

### Yield Protection (YP)
- Guarantees a yield level based on APH (Actual Production History)
- Indemnity = (guaranteed yield − actual yield) × projected price
- Does not respond to price declines — only yield shortfalls trigger payment
- Lower premium than RP; appropriate when price risk is managed separately

### Premium Subsidies
- Federal government subsidizes a portion of the premium at every coverage level
- Subsidy percentages (approximate): 50% level → 67%; 55% → 64%; 60% → 64%; 65% → 59%; 70% → 59%; 75% → 55%; 80% → 48%; 85% → 38%
- Enterprise units receive additional premium discount (~20% lower than optional units)
- A&O (administrative and operating) reimbursement paid to AIPs by USDA

### Actual Production History (APH)
- Yield database built from 4–10 years of producer records
- Transitional yields (T-yields) used when records are insufficient — typically 60–100% of county average
- Yield exclusion (YE) allows dropping years with county-wide disaster from APH
- Trend-adjusted APH available for some crops — adjusts historical yields upward for yield trend

## Pasture, Rangeland, Forage Insurance (PRF)
- Rainfall Index–based plan — no individual loss adjustment needed
- Coverage area divided into grid cells (~12 × 12 miles each)
- Indemnity triggered when actual rainfall index for the grid falls below the selected coverage level
- Producers select two-month index intervals that align with critical growing periods
- Must allocate coverage across at least two intervals (no more than specified per interval rules)
- Coverage levels: 70%–90% in 5% increments
- Productivity factor: 60%–150% to scale coverage to match operation's grazing value
- Premium subsidized at 51% (for 90% coverage) up to 59% (for 70% coverage)
- Sign-up deadline typically November 15 for the following year
- Does not cover hail, fire, or other non-rainfall perils
- Useful for cow-calf and stocker operations dependent on forage production

## Livestock Insurance Programs

### Livestock Risk Protection (LRP)
- Price insurance for fed cattle, feeder cattle, lamb, and swine
- Protects against price declines below a selected coverage price
- Coverage levels range from ~70% to ~100% of expected ending value
- Endorsement periods: 13, 17, 21, 26, 30, 34, 39, 43, 47, or 52 weeks
- Indemnity = (coverage price − actual ending value) × insured weight
- Premiums set daily based on CME futures and options; subsidized ~13–35% depending on coverage
- Purchased through approved livestock insurance agents
- No limit on number of head per endorsement; annual limits apply

### Livestock Gross Margin (LGM)
- Protects the gross margin (market value of livestock minus feed costs)
- Available for cattle, swine, and dairy
- Uses CME futures prices for livestock and feed to set expected and actual gross margins
- Indemnity paid when actual gross margin falls below guaranteed gross margin minus deductible
- Deductible chosen by producer ($0–$150/head for cattle)
- Monthly sales periods; coverage for up to 11 months
- Premium subsidized at approximately 18% (varies by species and coverage)

## ARC and PLC — Farm Bill Safety Net Programs

### Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC)
- County-level (ARC-CO) or individual (ARC-IC) revenue safety net
- ARC-CO guarantee = 86% of county benchmark revenue (5-year Olympic average yield × 5-year Olympic average price)
- Payment triggered when actual county revenue falls below the ARC-CO guarantee
- Payment limited to 10% of benchmark revenue per acre
- Applied to base acres, not planted acres
- ARC-IC uses individual whole-farm revenue across all covered commodities

### Price Loss Coverage (PLC)
- Price-based safety net; payment when national marketing year average price < reference price
- Payment rate = reference price − higher of (MYA price or loan rate)
- Applied to 85% of base acres × payment yield
- Reference prices set by statute (e.g., corn $3.70/bu, soybeans $8.40/bu, wheat $5.50/bu)
- Updated payment yields available under recent farm bills

### ARC/PLC and Crop Insurance Interaction
- Producers elect ARC or PLC on a commodity-by-commodity basis each farm bill cycle
- ARC-CO election: SCO (Supplemental Coverage Option) not available for that crop
- PLC election: SCO available — covers a band from 86% down to the producer's underlying coverage level
- ECO (Enhanced Coverage Option) available regardless of ARC/PLC election — covers 90% or 95% band
- Stacking ARC or PLC with crop insurance creates layered risk protection

## Whole-Farm Revenue Protection (WFRP)
- Insures total farm revenue from all commodities, including specialty crops, livestock, and nursery
- Designed for diversified operations and those without individual crop policies available
- Revenue guarantee based on 5-year farm tax records (Schedule F)
- Coverage levels: 50%–85%
- Diversification premium discount: more commodities → lower premium
- Covers revenue from crops, livestock, aquaculture, and direct-to-consumer sales
- Maximum insured revenue: $17 million (approximate current cap)
- Allows buy-up coverage and pairs with USDA marketing assistance loans

## Enterprise Risk Management Framework

### Risk Categories for Ag Businesses
- Production risk: weather, pests, disease, input quality
- Price/market risk: commodity price volatility, input cost swings, basis risk
- Financial risk: leverage, interest rates, liquidity, credit access
- Institutional/legal risk: regulation changes, trade policy, environmental compliance
- Human/personnel risk: key-person dependency, labor availability, health/safety
- Strategic risk: technology adoption, market shifts, competitive landscape

### Risk Assessment Process
- Identify and catalog all material risks across categories
- Estimate probability and financial impact of each risk (risk matrix)
- Prioritize risks by expected value of loss and operational criticality
- Assign risk owners and develop mitigation plans for top-tier risks
- Review and update risk register annually or after significant events

## Disaster Planning

### Drought
- Trigger points: soil moisture thresholds, pasture condition scores, reservoir levels
- Actions: reduce stocking rate early, secure alternative feed sources, activate PRF and crop insurance claims
- Maintain emergency water infrastructure (wells, pipelines, tanks)
- Document losses thoroughly for insurance and disaster assistance (LFP, ELAP, WHIP+)

### Flood
- Pre-position equipment and livestock to higher ground when warnings issued
- Maintain current floodplain maps and drainage system capacity
- Post-event: document damage, test water quality, assess soil contamination
- USDA Emergency Conservation Program (ECP) may fund debris removal and restoration

### Disease Outbreak (Livestock)
- Biosecurity protocols: visitor logs, boot baths, isolation pens, restricted access
- Rapid reporting to state veterinarian — federal indemnity programs require timely notification
- Quarantine and depopulation plans for high-consequence diseases (HPAI, FMD, ASF)
- Business interruption planning: how long can the operation survive with zero revenue?

### Market Collapse
- Stress-test cash flow at prices 30–50% below breakeven
- Maintain operating line of credit headroom for at least 6 months of fixed costs
- Diversify marketing channels and customer base
- Hedging program review: ensure coverage percentages align with production confidence

## Business Continuity Planning
- Identify critical functions: feeding/watering livestock, irrigation, grain drying, milking
- Power backup: generator sizing for essential loads, fuel supply contracts
- Key vendor and supply chain redundancy (feed, fuel, veterinary, parts)
- Communication plan: employee contact tree, customer/supplier notification protocols
- Data backup: farm management software, financial records, insurance documents (cloud + offsite)
- Annual tabletop exercises to test the plan with key personnel

## Succession Planning and Key-Person Risk
- Identify key individuals whose absence would disrupt operations (owner, herdsman, agronomist)
- Key-person life and disability insurance — benefit amount should cover 2–3 years of replacement cost
- Written succession plan: who takes over daily operations, who holds decision authority
- Buy-sell agreements funded by life insurance for multi-owner operations
- Estate planning coordination: wills, trusts, power of attorney, entity structure (LLC, S-corp, partnership)
- Gradual transition: mentoring, shared decision-making, phased ownership transfer
- Annual family/business meetings to review and update succession documents

## Liability Risk Management

### Farm Premises Liability
- General liability policy: $1–2 million per occurrence recommended minimum
- Umbrella/excess liability for operations with agritourism, farm stores, or public access
- Maintain safe premises: fencing, signage, hazard remediation, ADA considerations
- Workers' compensation: required in most states; covers employee injury claims

### Product Liability
- Relevant for direct-to-consumer sales, farmers markets, value-added products
- Product liability insurance or endorsement on commercial general liability
- Proper labeling, allergen disclosure, food safety certifications (GAP, FSMA compliance)
- Recall plan and traceability systems for all products sold

### Environmental Liability
- Pollution liability coverage for chemical storage, fuel tanks, manure management
- Nutrient management plans to comply with Clean Water Act and state regulations
- SPCC plans (Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure) for fuel storage >1,320 gallons
- CERCLA/Superfund awareness for operations near contaminated sites
- Pesticide applicator licensing and drift mitigation protocols

## Cyber Risk for Modern Ag Operations
- Precision ag platforms, GPS-guided equipment, and IoT sensors expand attack surface
- Ransomware risk: locked-out grain elevators, disabled precision planting systems
- Phishing attacks targeting farm office email for wire fraud
- Protect farm management and financial software with strong passwords and MFA
- Cyber liability insurance: covers data breach response, business interruption, ransom payments
- Regular software updates and patching on all connected equipment
- Employee training on recognizing phishing and social engineering
- Vendor security assessment for cloud-based ag platforms and data providers
- Incident response plan: who to call, how to isolate systems, backup restoration procedure

## Weather Risk Management Tools Beyond Insurance
- Commodity hedging: futures and options on CME/CBOT to lock in prices or set price floors
- Forward contracts with elevators or end-users — lock in basis and flat price
- Weather derivatives: contracts that pay based on temperature, rainfall, or growing degree days
- Irrigation infrastructure: pivot, drip, or flood systems to mitigate drought risk
- Diversification: multiple crops, livestock species, geographic spread across counties
- Soil health practices: cover crops, no-till, and organic matter building to improve water-holding capacity
- On-farm grain storage: flexibility to market grain post-harvest and avoid basis pressure
- Market intelligence subscriptions: USDA reports, private forecasts, basis monitoring tools
- Scenario planning: model operation financials under best-case, expected, and worst-case weather/price
