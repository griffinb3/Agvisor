# Federal Crop Insurance Program Guide

## Overview
The Federal Crop Insurance Program, administered by USDA's Risk Management Agency (RMA), helps farmers manage risk through subsidized insurance products. Policies are sold and serviced by private insurance companies known as Approved Insurance Providers (AIPs). The federal government subsidizes a portion of the premium, making coverage affordable for producers. Over 90% of eligible cropland in the US is insured under the program. The Federal Crop Insurance Corporation (FCIC) sets program rules and premium rates.

## Catastrophic Risk Protection (CAT)
CAT coverage is the minimum level of crop insurance, covering 50% of the approved yield at 55% of the projected price. There is no premium cost to the producer, only a $655 administrative fee per crop per county (waived for limited resource, beginning, and socially disadvantaged farmers). CAT coverage must be obtained at the local FSA office. While basic, CAT coverage is required for eligibility in certain USDA disaster assistance programs.

## Yield-Based Policies
Multi-Peril Crop Insurance (MPCI) protects against losses from natural causes including drought, excessive moisture, hail, wind, frost, freeze, insects, and disease. Coverage levels range from 50% to 85% of the approved yield in 5% increments. Higher coverage levels have lower premium subsidies but provide more protection. Yield Protection (YP) covers only yield losses. The indemnity is calculated as the guarantee minus actual production multiplied by the projected price.

## Revenue Protection (RP)
Revenue Protection is the most popular crop insurance product, covering over 80% of insured acres. RP guarantees a level of revenue based on the higher of the projected price or harvest price multiplied by the producer's actual production history (APH) yield. This harvest price option increases the guarantee if harvest prices exceed projected prices, protecting the replacement cost of lost production. Revenue Protection with Harvest Price Exclusion (RP-HPE) is a lower-cost alternative that does not include the harvest price option.

Projected prices are established using futures market prices during the discovery period (typically February for spring crops). Harvest prices are similarly established using futures during the harvest price discovery period (typically October for corn and soybeans). The revenue guarantee equals the coverage level multiplied by the projected price multiplied by the APH yield.

## Area-Based Plans
Area Risk Protection Insurance (ARPI) bases coverage on expected county yields or revenue rather than individual farm experience. Area Yield Protection (AYP) triggers payments when county yields fall below the trigger level. Area Revenue Protection (ARP) triggers when county revenue falls below the trigger level. Coverage levels range from 70% to 90% of expected county performance.

Premiums are generally lower than individual plans because county-level coverage carries less basis risk for the insurer. However, individual farm losses may not match county-level losses, creating basis risk for the producer. ARPI may be a good choice for farms with yields that are highly correlated with county averages, or as a supplement to individual coverage on separate farm units.

## Whole-Farm Revenue Protection (WFRP)
WFRP provides coverage for all commodities on the farm under one policy, making it well-suited for diversified farms, specialty crop operations, and farms producing commodities for which individual crop insurance is not available. Coverage is based on the farm's Schedule F tax return history for the previous 5 years. Maximum insurable revenue is $17 million.

WFRP recognizes diversification as a risk management tool by offering premium discounts of up to 21% for operations with multiple commodities. Coverage levels range from 50% to 85% of expected revenue. WFRP covers revenue from commodity sales, value-added products, and direct-to-consumer sales. It can also cover animals and animal products.

## Key Deadlines
Sales closing dates are the last day to purchase or change crop insurance coverage. Common deadlines include March 15 for spring-planted crops in most states and September 30 for fall-planted small grains. These dates vary by crop and county. Premium billing dates are typically August-October. Acreage reports are due by July 15 for most spring crops and November 15 for fall crops. Production reports are due by the acreage reporting date of the following year.

## Premium Subsidies
The federal government subsidizes crop insurance premiums significantly. Subsidy percentages by coverage level: 50% coverage receives 67% subsidy, 55% coverage receives 64% subsidy, 60% coverage receives 64% subsidy, 65% coverage receives 59% subsidy, 70% coverage receives 59% subsidy, 75% coverage receives 55% subsidy, 80% coverage receives 48% subsidy, and 85% coverage receives 38% subsidy. Beginning farmers and ranchers receive an additional 10 percentage points of premium subsidy for the first 5 years of crop insurance coverage.

## Actual Production History (APH)
A producer's APH yield is calculated using 4-10 years of actual yield records. If fewer than 4 years of records are available, transitional yields (T-yields) based on county averages are used to fill the database. T-yields are set at percentages of the county expected yield. Maintaining accurate production records is essential for establishing and maintaining an accurate APH database. Yield exclusions allow producers to exclude yields from years with widespread natural disasters. Yield adjustment provisions prevent APH yields from declining too rapidly.

## Prevented Planting
If a producer is prevented from planting an insured crop by a covered cause of loss by the final planting date, prevented planting coverage pays 60% of the production guarantee for the prevented acreage (55% for some crops). Producers with buy-up coverage above the catastrophic level receive prevented planting coverage automatically. A producer must have intended and been able to plant the crop but was prevented by an insurable cause. Eligible causes include excess moisture, flooding, drought, and other natural disasters. Producers who plant a cover crop on prevented planting acres may still receive the full prevented planting payment.

## Filing a Claim
When crop losses occur, producers must notify their insurance agent within 72 hours of discovery of damage, or within 72 hours of harvest for in-field losses discovered during harvest. Do not destroy evidence of loss before an adjuster inspects the damage. A loss adjuster will inspect the damage, verify the cause of loss, and calculate the indemnity based on the appraised or harvested production. Claims are typically paid within 30 days of the final loss adjustment. Producers should maintain thorough production and harvest records to support claims. Late-reported claims may be denied.
