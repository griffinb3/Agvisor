# Risk Management and Insurance Programs for Agriculture

## Types of Agricultural Risk

### Production Risk
Production risk arises from the uncertainty of natural events affecting crop yields and livestock performance. Weather events including drought, excessive rainfall, flooding, hail, frost, freeze, hurricanes, and tornadoes are primary production risks. Pest pressures from insects, diseases, and weeds add variability. Input quality issues including seed germination, fertilizer effectiveness, and equipment reliability also contribute. Mitigation strategies include crop insurance, enterprise diversification, irrigation, integrated pest management, variety and hybrid selection for stress tolerance, and maintaining equipment in reliable condition.

### Price and Market Risk
Price risk results from commodity price volatility driven by global supply and demand dynamics, trade policies and tariffs, weather events in major producing regions, currency exchange rate fluctuations, energy prices, and government policies. Agricultural commodity prices can move 20-40% within a single marketing year.

Tools for managing price risk include futures and options hedging on commodity exchanges, forward cash contracts with buyers, marketing cooperatives that pool and market member production, USDA safety net programs including Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC) and Price Loss Coverage (PLC), crop revenue insurance products, and diversification across commodities with uncorrelated price movements.

### Financial Risk
Financial risk relates to the ability to meet financial obligations and maintain the financial health of the operation. High leverage (debt relative to assets), variable interest rates, cash flow mismatches between when expenses occur and income is received, and over-investment in fixed assets increase financial risk.

Managing financial risk involves maintaining adequate working capital (current ratio above 1.5), matching loan terms and repayment schedules to the useful life and income generation pattern of financed assets, building cash reserves equivalent to 3-6 months of operating expenses, stress-testing financial plans against scenarios of lower prices, reduced yields, and higher interest rates, maintaining a borrowing reserve for unexpected needs, and avoiding emotional or speculative financial decisions.

### Institutional and Legal Risk
Changes in government policies, environmental regulations, tax laws, labor laws, trade agreements, and zoning ordinances can significantly affect farm profitability and operations. Institutional risks are difficult to predict but can be partially managed by staying informed of policy discussions, participating in commodity organizations and farm groups that engage in policy advocacy, maintaining regulatory compliance with documentation, building flexibility into business plans, and consulting with agricultural attorneys on legal matters.

### Human and Personal Risk
Illness, injury, death, disability, divorce, and family relationship conflicts can disrupt farm operations and threaten business continuity. Human risk management tools include adequate life insurance (term and whole life) on key persons, disability income insurance to replace lost earnings, health insurance for the farm family, key person agreements that document roles and compensation, succession plans that address unexpected death or disability, estate plans including wills, powers of attorney, and healthcare directives, and maintaining open family communication about business plans and expectations.

## USDA Safety Net Programs

### Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC)
ARC provides revenue-based support when actual county crop revenue falls below the ARC guarantee. The ARC guarantee is calculated as 86% of the benchmark revenue, which is the 5-year Olympic average of county yields multiplied by the 5-year Olympic average of national marketing year average prices. When actual county revenue falls below the guarantee, ARC-CO payments equal the difference, capped at 10% of benchmark revenue.

ARC is available on a county basis (ARC-CO, most popular) or individual farm basis (ARC-IC). ARC-CO calculations use county-level yields while ARC-IC uses the individual farm's actual yields across all covered commodities. Payments are made on 85% of base acres for ARC-CO and 65% of base acres for ARC-IC. Producers make an annual election between ARC and PLC for each commodity on each farm.

### Price Loss Coverage (PLC)
PLC provides payments when the national marketing year average (MYA) price for a covered commodity falls below its statutory reference price. Reference prices include corn at $3.70 per bushel, soybeans at $8.40 per bushel, wheat at $5.50 per bushel, grain sorghum at $3.95 per bushel, barley at $4.95 per bushel, oats at $2.40 per bushel, and rice at $14.00 per hundredweight.

PLC payments are calculated as the difference between the reference price and the MYA price (or the effective reference price, which is the higher of the statutory reference price or 85% of the Olympic average price) multiplied by 85% of base acres multiplied by the program yield for the farm. PLC provides the most protection when prices are significantly below reference prices.

### ARC vs PLC Decision
The optimal choice between ARC and PLC depends on expected prices, expected yields, and historical benchmarks. ARC tends to perform better when prices are near or moderately above reference prices but below recent historical averages, because the revenue guarantee adjusts with market conditions. PLC tends to perform better when prices are expected to fall significantly below reference prices, because the reference price provides a fixed floor.

USDA offers a free online decision tool, and university extension services provide analysis tools including the Gardner Policy Institute tool and state-specific analysis. Elections should be made on a commodity-by-commodity, farm-by-farm basis after analyzing expected returns under each option. The election is made annually during the signup period.

## Livestock Risk Protection (LRP) Insurance
LRP provides protection against declining livestock prices for fed cattle, feeder cattle, lamb, and swine. Producers select a coverage price (from available options based on CME futures and options), number of head, and insurance period (13-52 weeks for cattle, 13-26 weeks for swine). If the actual ending value (based on CME prices at the end of the coverage period) is below the coverage price, an indemnity is paid on a per-head basis.

LRP is subsidized by the federal government with subsidies of 13-35% depending on coverage level. Coverage can be purchased daily through livestock crop insurance agents. LRP is available for up to 12,000 head of cattle, 40,000 head of swine, and 10,000 head of lambs per crop year. LRP is an accessible price risk management tool for producers who do not want to trade futures and options directly.

## Dairy Margin Coverage (DMC)
DMC protects dairy producers when the margin between the all-milk price and the average feed cost falls below a selected coverage level. The margin is calculated monthly using the USDA announced all-milk price minus the DMC feed cost (corn price times 1.0728, soybean meal price times 0.00735, and alfalfa hay price times 0.0137).

Coverage levels range from $4.00 to $9.50 per hundredweight in $0.50 increments. Producers can select coverage on 5-95% of their production history in 5% increments. Tier 1 coverage on the first 5 million lbs of production history receives significantly higher premium subsidies than Tier 2 coverage on additional production. The $4.00 coverage level has zero premium (catastrophic coverage). DMC signup occurs annually at the local FSA office. Supplemental DMC provides additional coverage for small and mid-size dairy operations based on production history increases.

## Noninsured Crop Disaster Assistance Program (NAP)
NAP provides financial assistance to producers of crops for which federal crop insurance is not available, including many specialty crops, forage crops, and non-traditional commodities. Basic NAP coverage (50% of expected production at 55% of average market price) is available at no cost to eligible producers (only a $325 service fee per crop per county, waived for limited resource, beginning, and socially disadvantaged producers).

Buy-up coverage levels from 50% to 65% of expected production at 100% of average market price are available for additional premium. Application must be filed by the applicable deadline before the crop is planted or before the coverage period begins for perennial crops. NAP coverage requires that producers maintain accurate production records.

## Diversification as Risk Management
Diversifying across multiple crops, livestock species, and revenue sources reduces overall farm risk by ensuring that a single adverse event does not impact all income sources simultaneously. Types of diversification include enterprise diversification (producing multiple commodities), geographic diversification (farming in multiple locations with different microclimates), vertical integration (value-added processing and direct marketing), temporal diversification (marketing production across multiple time periods), and off-farm income diversification. Research shows that diversified farms have lower income variability and lower rates of financial stress than highly specialized operations.
