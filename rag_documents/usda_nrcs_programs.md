# USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) Programs

## Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP)
EQIP provides financial and technical assistance to agricultural producers to address natural resource concerns on their land. Payments can cover up to 75% of the cost of conservation practices, with higher payment rates up to 90% available for beginning, socially disadvantaged, limited resource, or veteran farmers and ranchers. Common practices funded by EQIP include cover crops, nutrient management plans, irrigation efficiency upgrades, prescribed grazing systems, pollinator habitat establishment, high tunnels for season extension, livestock waste management facilities, and fencing for rotational grazing.

Applications are accepted year-round but ranked in funding cycles, typically with application deadlines in fall and early winter. Contracts can be up to 10 years in duration. EQIP has special initiatives including the Organic Initiative for transitioning and certified organic producers, the National Water Quality Initiative targeting priority watersheds, the Conservation Innovation Grants program for testing new approaches, and the On-Farm Energy Initiative for energy audits and efficiency improvements. Payment schedules and eligible practices vary by state. Producers should work with their local NRCS office early in the planning process.

## Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP)
CSP helps agricultural producers maintain and improve existing conservation systems and adopt additional conservation activities on their operations. It is the largest conservation program by acreage in the United States, covering over 70 million acres. Unlike EQIP which funds new practice adoption, CSP rewards producers for their existing stewardship and incentivizes enhancement of those practices.

Participants earn annual payments based on their conservation performance, calculated using the Conservation Measurement Tool (CMT). Payments are made on a per-acre basis. Contracts are 5 years and can be renewed for an additional 5 years. Enhancements available under CSP include advanced nutrient management, improved cover crop systems, precision agriculture technology adoption, pollinator habitat, enhanced rotational grazing, and energy conservation measures. Minimum eligibility requires demonstration of existing conservation on the operation.

## Agricultural Conservation Easement Program (ACEP)
ACEP helps landowners, land trusts, and other entities protect, restore, and enhance wetlands, grasslands, and working farms through conservation easements. The program has two components. Agricultural Land Easements (ALE) protect farmland from conversion to non-agricultural uses including development. NRCS contributes up to 50% of the fair market value of the easement (up to 75% for grasslands of special environmental significance).

Wetland Reserve Easements (WRE) restore and protect wetlands on agricultural land. Options include permanent easements (100% of easement value plus 100% restoration costs), 30-year easements (50-75% of permanent easement value plus 50-75% of restoration costs), and term easements (50-75% of permanent value plus 50-75% restoration costs). Eligible land includes farmed or converted wetlands that can be successfully and cost-effectively restored. WRE supports critical habitat for migratory birds and other wildlife.

## Regional Conservation Partnership Program (RCPP)
RCPP promotes coordination of conservation activities at the landscape level by leveraging federal investment with partner contributions. Partners may include state and local governments, farmer cooperatives, water districts, conservation districts, non-profit organizations, and institutions of higher education. RCPP projects address specific resource concerns within defined geographic areas.

Partners contribute at least dollar-for-dollar match of NRCS funding through cash, in-kind services, or technical assistance. Critical Conservation Areas receiving targeted RCPP funding include the Chesapeake Bay watershed, Great Lakes region, Mississippi River Basin, Colorado River Basin, and the Longleaf Pine range. RCPP agreements are typically 5 years with possible extensions.

## Conservation Technical Assistance
NRCS provides free technical assistance to all landowners regardless of participation in financial programs. Services include soil surveys and interpretations, resource assessments and inventories, conservation planning, engineering designs for structural practices, and agronomic recommendations. Conservation planners work one-on-one with producers to develop comprehensive conservation plans addressing soil health, water quality, wildlife habitat, and other resource concerns.

Conservation District offices serve as the local delivery point for NRCS programs and services. Over 3,000 conservation districts across the country provide local governance and support for conservation efforts. Producers can schedule appointments with NRCS staff through their local conservation district office.

## Application Process and Tips
Farmers should contact their local NRCS office or conservation district to begin the application process. A resource assessment of the farm is typically the first step. Applications are ranked based on environmental benefits, cost effectiveness, program priorities, and national and state ranking criteria. It is recommended to apply early and work with NRCS staff to develop a comprehensive conservation plan before submitting applications. Having a completed conservation plan strengthens applications for all NRCS programs. Producers are encouraged to apply even if not immediately funded, as applications can be reconsidered in subsequent funding cycles.
