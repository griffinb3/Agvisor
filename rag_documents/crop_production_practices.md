# Crop Production Best Practices by Region

## Corn Belt (Midwest) Production
The Corn Belt includes Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, Minnesota, Wisconsin, and parts of Missouri, Nebraska, South Dakota, and Michigan. Corn planting typically begins in mid-April and continues through late May, with optimal planting windows varying by latitude and soil conditions. Target plant populations range from 30,000 to 36,000 seeds per acre depending on soil productivity, moisture availability, and hybrid characteristics. Higher populations are appropriate on highly productive soils with reliable moisture.

Nitrogen management is critical for profitable corn production. Application rates typically range from 140-200 lbs per acre based on yield goals, previous crop, and soil nitrogen credits. The Maximum Return to Nitrogen (MRTN) approach optimizes rates based on the corn-to-nitrogen price ratio. Split applications (pre-plant plus sidedress) improve nitrogen use efficiency by 10-15% compared to single pre-plant applications. Nitrification inhibitors and enhanced efficiency fertilizers reduce nitrogen losses from leaching and denitrification.

Soybean production in the region follows corn in most rotations. Planting occurs from late April through June, with earlier planting generally producing higher yields due to longer vegetative growth and more nodes. Seeding rates of 120,000-140,000 seeds per acre are common for 30-inch rows, with higher rates for narrow rows or drilled soybeans. Inoculant use ensures proper nitrogen fixation, especially in fields with no recent soybean history. Weed management programs should include both pre-emergence and post-emergence herbicides timed to weed size. Resistance management requires rotating herbicide modes of action.

## Southern Plains Production
The Southern Plains (Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas) features diverse cropping systems including wheat, cotton, grain sorghum, irrigated corn, and specialty crops. Winter wheat is planted September through October with harvest in May-June. Dual-purpose wheat (grazing and grain) is common in Oklahoma and Texas, with cattle removed by first hollow stem to avoid yield reduction.

Cotton planting begins in May when soil temperatures reach 65°F at 4-inch depth for 3 consecutive days. Plant populations of 35,000-50,000 plants per acre are typical depending on irrigation and rainfall zone. Growth regulators (mepiquat chloride) manage vegetative growth in high-yield environments. Defoliation timing based on nodes above cracked boll and percent open bolls determines harvest timing.

Grain sorghum is a heat and drought-tolerant alternative to corn for dryland production, requiring 30% less water than corn. Planting occurs when soil temperatures reach 60°F. Seeding rates of 40,000-80,000 seeds per acre vary by moisture zone. Sorghum is an excellent rotational crop that breaks pest cycles and improves soil structure.

Water management is critical in the region with declining Ogallala Aquifer levels. Irrigation allocation strategies prioritize water to the most responsive growth stages. Deficit irrigation applies less than full crop water requirement, accepting some yield reduction to extend aquifer life. Subsurface drip irrigation systems improve water use efficiency by 15-25% compared to center pivot systems but require higher capital investment.

## Southeast Production
The Southeast (Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Carolinas, Virginia) produces cotton, peanuts, soybeans, corn, tobacco, specialty crops, and tree fruits. Longer growing seasons allow double-cropping systems such as winter wheat followed by soybeans. Disease and insect pressure is higher due to warm, humid conditions, requiring robust integrated pest management approaches.

Cover crop adoption is increasing in the Southeast with crimson clover, winter rye, radishes, and multi-species mixes being popular choices. Cover crops in this region provide excellent erosion control, weed suppression, and nitrogen fixation (legume covers). Termination timing is critical as cover crops must be killed 2-3 weeks before cash crop planting to avoid moisture competition and pest harboring.

Peanut production requires specific soil types (sandy loam) and disease management programs. Crop rotation with at least 2 years between peanut crops reduces soilborne disease pressure. Irrigation management during pegging and pod fill is critical for yield and quality. Peanut variety selection should consider disease resistance, maturity, and market type (runner, Virginia, Spanish, Valencia).

## Pacific Northwest and Western Production
The Pacific Northwest (Washington, Oregon, Idaho) produces wheat, potatoes, apples, cherries, wine grapes, hops, and other specialty crops. Irrigated agriculture dominates in the arid regions east of the Cascades. Center pivot and linear move irrigation systems are widely used. Dryland wheat-fallow rotations are common in lower rainfall areas, with annual cropping possible where precipitation exceeds 16 inches.

California's Central Valley produces over 250 different crops and is the most productive agricultural region in the world by value. Permanent crops (almonds, pistachios, walnuts, grapes, citrus) require long-term investment and irrigation commitments. Water availability and cost are primary production constraints, with ongoing competition between agricultural, urban, and environmental water uses.

Precision agriculture adoption is high across western production regions. Technologies including variable rate irrigation, GPS-guided auto-steer, aerial imagery, and yield monitoring are widely utilized to optimize inputs and manage spatial variability within fields.

## Soil Health Principles
Five principles of soil health apply across all regions. First, minimize soil disturbance through reduced tillage or no-till practices to preserve soil structure and biological activity. Second, maximize soil cover using crop residue management and cover crops to reduce erosion and moderate soil temperature. Third, maximize biodiversity through diverse crop rotations and cover crop mixes to support varied soil biological communities. Fourth, maintain living roots year-round to feed soil organisms and maintain soil structure. Fifth, integrate livestock where possible to cycle nutrients and stimulate plant growth through managed grazing.

Implementing these principles simultaneously improves water infiltration by 25-50%, reduces erosion by 50-90%, enhances nutrient cycling efficiency, and builds soil organic matter by 0.1-0.3% over 5-10 years. Soil health practices reduce input costs over time as biological nutrient cycling replaces purchased inputs and improved water holding capacity reduces drought stress.

## Integrated Pest Management (IPM)
IPM combines biological, cultural, physical, and chemical tools to manage pests effectively while minimizing economic, health, and environmental risks. Key IPM practices include scouting fields regularly on a defined schedule, using economic thresholds to guide treatment decisions rather than calendar-based applications, rotating herbicide modes of action to prevent and manage resistance, utilizing beneficial insects and biological controls, maintaining detailed records of pest pressure and treatment outcomes, and staying current on pest identification and management through extension resources.

Herbicide resistance is a growing challenge across all crop production regions. Glyphosate-resistant weeds including Palmer amaranth, waterhemp, horseweed, and Italian ryegrass require diversified management strategies. Effective resistance management combines multiple herbicide modes of action, cultural practices (cover crops, competitive crop varieties, narrow rows), mechanical control, and harvest weed seed management.

## Precision Agriculture Technologies
GPS-guided auto-steer systems improve planting accuracy and reduce overlap, saving seed and input costs by 3-5%. Variable rate application of seed, fertilizer, and chemicals matches inputs to field variability, improving efficiency and reducing environmental impact. Yield monitors for harvest data collection enable multi-year yield mapping that drives variable rate prescriptions. Drone and satellite imagery support in-season crop scouting and stress detection. Soil electrical conductivity mapping delineates management zones based on soil texture and properties. Moisture sensors and weather-based models guide irrigation scheduling for optimal water use efficiency. Return on investment varies by technology and farm size but generally favors operations over 500 acres for most technologies.
