# USDA Farm Service Agency (FSA) Loan Programs

## Direct Farm Ownership Loans
FSA provides Direct Farm Ownership Loans to help farmers and ranchers purchase or enlarge a farm or ranch, construct new buildings or improve existing ones, pay closing costs, and promote soil and water conservation. The maximum loan amount is $600,000. Interest rates are set periodically based on the government's cost of borrowing. Terms can be up to 40 years. Applicants must be unable to obtain credit elsewhere at reasonable rates and terms. Applicants must have sufficient farm management experience or education. The land purchased must not exceed the median farm size in the county.

## Direct Operating Loans
Direct Operating Loans help farmers and ranchers purchase livestock, farm equipment, feed, seed, fuel, insurance, and other operating expenses. They can also be used to pay for minor improvements to buildings, costs associated with land and water development, family living expenses, and refinancing debts under certain conditions. The maximum loan amount is $400,000 with repayment terms typically from 1 to 7 years. Operating loans for annual expenses are typically repaid within 12 months. Equipment loans may have terms up to 7 years. Applicants must show an ability to repay the loan from farm income.

## Microloans
FSA Microloans provide up to $50,000 for small and beginning farmers. They feature a simplified application process with less paperwork than traditional FSA loans. Microloans can be used for operating expenses including seed, fertilizer, livestock feed, equipment, and living expenses during the production cycle. Ownership microloans can be used to purchase land or make improvements. The streamlined process requires fewer forms and allows alternative documentation of farm experience, including experience managing non-farm businesses. Microloans are especially popular among specialty crop, niche, and direct-market producers. There is no requirement to have been turned down by a commercial lender.

## Emergency Loans
FSA Emergency Loans help producers recover from production and physical losses due to drought, flooding, other natural disasters, or quarantine. The maximum loan amount is $500,000. Applicants must have suffered at least a 30% loss in crop production or a physical loss to livestock, livestock products, real estate, or chattel property. The county must be declared a disaster area by the President or designated by the Secretary of Agriculture. Emergency loan funds may be used to restore or replace essential property, pay production costs associated with the disaster year, pay essential family living expenses, reorganize the farming operation, and refinance certain debts.

## Guaranteed Farm Loans
FSA guarantees loans made by conventional lenders (banks, credit unions, Farm Credit System) to farmers who do not qualify for standard commercial credit. FSA guarantees up to 95% of the loss of principal and interest on a loan. Maximum guaranteed loan amounts are $1,825,000 for farm ownership and $1,825,000 for operating purposes. This reduces the lender's risk and makes credit available to farmers who might otherwise be denied. Interest rates are negotiated between the borrower and lender but must be reasonable. Guaranteed loans often provide faster processing than direct loans.

## Youth Loans
Youth Loans are available to rural youth ages 10-20 to establish and operate income-producing agricultural projects. The maximum loan amount is $5,000. Projects must be of modest size and educational in nature, operated under the supervision of an adult advisor such as a 4-H or FFA leader, extension agent, or vocational agriculture teacher. Projects can include raising livestock, growing crops, or other agricultural activities. The youth must be a member of a 4-H club, FFA chapter, or similar organization.

## Eligibility Requirements
General eligibility for FSA loans includes U.S. citizenship or legal resident status, sufficient education, training, or at least 3 years of experience managing a farm, an acceptable credit history demonstrating willingness to repay debts, inability to obtain credit elsewhere at reasonable rates for direct loans, legal capacity to incur debt, compliance with highly erodible land conservation and wetland conservation provisions, and no conviction of controlled substance violations. Applicants must also not be delinquent on any federal debt. Previous recipients of FSA loan servicing including write-downs must wait 3 years before reapplying for certain loan types.

## Beginning Farmer Provisions
FSA targets a portion of its direct and guaranteed loan funds to beginning farmers and ranchers. Beginning farmers are defined as those who have operated a farm for not more than 10 years, do not own a farm greater than 30% of the average size farm in the county, and meet other eligibility requirements. Down payment requirements are reduced to 5% for direct farm ownership loans. Interest rates may be lower. The Down Payment Loan program partners FSA with commercial lenders, with FSA providing up to 45% financing and requiring only 5% from the borrower. Beginning farmers receive preference in loan processing and allocation of limited funds.
