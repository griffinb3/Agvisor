# Livestock Management Reference Guide

## Beef Cattle Management

### Cow-Calf Operations
Successful cow-calf operations require attention to nutrition, breeding management, calving management, and herd health. Calving season should be timed to match forage availability and market timing, with spring calving (February-April) being most common in the northern US and fall calving (September-November) in parts of the South. Body condition scoring (BCS) is critical for reproductive success, with cows needing to be at BCS 5-6 on a 9-point scale at calving time. Thin cows at calving (BCS 4 or below) will have extended postpartum intervals and reduced rebreeding rates.

Nutrition management centers on matching cow nutritional requirements to forage quality and supplementation. Nutritional demands peak during late gestation and early lactation. Protein supplementation is often the first limiting nutrient in winter feeding programs based on dormant forages. Mineral supplementation should address regional deficiencies and include salt, calcium, phosphorus, and trace minerals (copper, zinc, selenium, manganese). Free-choice mineral programs should target intake of 2-4 ounces per head per day.

### Breeding and Genetics
Breeding season should be limited to 60-90 days to produce a uniform calf crop with calves of similar age and weight at weaning. Bull-to-cow ratios of 1:20 to 1:25 are typical for mature bulls in pasture breeding systems. Yearling bulls should be exposed to fewer females (1:15 ratio). Breeding soundness exams should be performed 30-60 days before the breeding season to identify subfertile bulls.

Expected Progeny Differences (EPDs) should guide bull selection based on herd goals. Key EPDs include calving ease direct (for heifer breeding), birth weight, weaning weight, yearling weight, milk (maternal weaning weight), and carcass traits (marbling, ribeye area, fat thickness). Selection indices combine multiple EPDs into economically weighted values that simplify selection decisions. Genomic-enhanced EPDs improve accuracy, especially for young bulls with limited progeny data.

### Stocker and Backgrounding Operations
Stocker operations purchase lightweight calves (300-500 lbs) and grow them on grass or mixed rations to 600-800 lbs before selling to feedlots. Success depends on cattle procurement, health management at arrival, nutrition management, and marketing timing. Processing at arrival should include vaccination (respiratory complex, clostridial diseases), deworming, implanting, and treatment of arriving sick cattle.

Health management at receiving is critical as newly weaned, commingled, and transported calves are highly susceptible to bovine respiratory disease (BRD). Preconditioning programs that wean, vaccinate, and bunk-break calves on the ranch of origin reduce health risk and command price premiums. Target gains of 1.5-2.5 lbs per day depend on the feeding program (pasture vs backgrounding ration). Timing purchases and sales to capture seasonal price spreads is essential for profitability.

### Feedlot Finishing
Feedlot operations finish cattle to harvest weight (1,200-1,400 lbs) on high-energy grain-based rations. Step-up feeding programs gradually transition cattle from roughage-based to grain-based diets over 21-28 days using 3-5 ration steps. Rapid transitions increase risk of acidosis and liver abscesses. Feed conversion ratios typically range from 5.5:1 to 7:1 (lbs feed per lb gain), varying with cattle type, ration energy density, and weather conditions.

Growth-promoting technologies including implants, ionophores (monensin, lasalocid), and beta-agonists (ractopamine, zilpaterol) improve feed efficiency and average daily gain by 10-20%. Understanding basis patterns, futures market hedging, and cattle market cycles is essential for managing price risk and profitability in feeding operations. Cost of gain analysis determines whether it is more profitable to market cattle at lighter or heavier weights.

## Dairy Operations
Dairy management centers on nutrition, reproduction, udder health, and facility management. Average milk production in the US exceeds 24,000 lbs per cow per year, with top herds exceeding 30,000 lbs. Total Mixed Rations (TMR) balance forages (corn silage, alfalfa hay, grass silage), energy sources (corn, barley), protein supplements (soybean meal, canola meal, distillers grains), minerals, and vitamins to meet the cow's requirements at each stage of lactation.

Reproduction goals include 12-13 month calving intervals, voluntary waiting periods of 50-70 days post-calving, conception rates above 35%, and pregnancy rates above 20%. Synchronization and timed artificial insemination (TAI) protocols improve reproductive efficiency by ensuring cows are inseminated on schedule. Genomic testing of dairy heifers allows genetic selection and culling decisions to be made before the animal enters the milking herd.

Somatic cell counts (SCC) below 200,000 cells/mL indicate good udder health and milk quality. SCC above 400,000 reduces milk production and results in milk quality premiums being lost. Mastitis prevention includes proper milking procedures, teat dipping, dry cow therapy, clean housing, and culling of chronically infected cows. Bulk tank SCC monitoring and individual cow culture testing guide mastitis management decisions.

## Swine Production
Modern swine production is largely vertically integrated with contract growing arrangements. Farrow-to-finish operations manage breeding, gestation, farrowing, nursery, growing, and finishing phases. Key performance metrics include pigs weaned per sow per year (target 25-30), pre-weaning mortality (target below 10%), nursery mortality (target below 3%), feed conversion in finishing (2.5-2.8 lbs feed per lb gain), and average daily gain in finishing (1.8-2.2 lbs per day).

Biosecurity is the most critical management practice in swine production for preventing introduction and spread of diseases including Porcine Reproductive and Respiratory Syndrome (PRRS), Porcine Epidemic Diarrhea (PEDv), and African Swine Fever (ASF). Biosecurity protocols include controlled access, shower-in/shower-out procedures, downtime requirements, load-out biosecurity, gilt acclimation protocols, and feed biosecurity. Site-specific biosecurity plans should be developed with veterinary guidance.

## Poultry Production
Broiler production involves growing chickens from day-old chicks to market weight (5-9 lbs live weight) in 35-63 days depending on target weight. Contract growers provide housing, labor, utilities, and litter management while integrators provide birds, feed, medications, field service, and processing and marketing. Key performance metrics include feed conversion ratio (1.6-1.9 lbs feed per lb live weight), livability above 95%, average daily gain, and condemnation rates.

House environmental management is critical for bird performance, welfare, and grower pay. Minimum ventilation maintains air quality (ammonia below 25 ppm) while conserving heat. Transitional ventilation manages increasing bird heat production. Tunnel ventilation with evaporative cooling pads manages heat stress during summer. Litter moisture management (target 20-25%) prevents footpad dermatitis and ammonia generation.

## Pasture and Forage Management
Rotational grazing improves pasture productivity by 25-50% compared to continuous grazing by allowing forage plants adequate rest and recovery between grazing events. Management Intensive Rotational Grazing (MIRG) involves short grazing periods (1-3 days) followed by adequate rest periods (21-45 days in spring, 45-60 days in summer and fall). Stocking rate should balance animal nutritional needs with pasture growth rates throughout the growing season.

Forage testing guides supplementation decisions by identifying nutrient deficiencies in the available forage. Key analyses include crude protein, total digestible nutrients (TDN), acid detergent fiber (ADF), neutral detergent fiber (NDF), and mineral content. Stockpiling tall fescue for fall and winter grazing reduces hay feeding costs by 30-60 days in the eastern US. Strip grazing stockpiled fescue improves utilization efficiency.

Hay production requires timely harvest to balance yield and quality. First cutting alfalfa should be harvested at early bloom (10% bloom) for dairy-quality hay. Grass hay quality is highest when cut at the boot to early heading stage. Rain damage reduces hay quality significantly, making weather monitoring essential for harvest timing. Wrapped baleage (fermented forage) eliminates weather risk and preserves forage quality but requires higher equipment investment.

## Animal Health Programs
Preventive health programs are more cost-effective than treating illness after it occurs. Essential components include vaccination protocols tailored to regional disease risks and herd-specific history, parasite control programs based on fecal egg counts rather than calendar-based deworming, biosecurity measures to prevent disease introduction including quarantine of new arrivals, working relationships with a licensed veterinarian familiar with the operation, and comprehensive record-keeping systems for individual animal health events.

The Veterinary Feed Directive (VFD) requires veterinary oversight for use of medically important antibiotics in feed and water. A valid veterinarian-client-patient relationship (VCPR) must be established, and the veterinarian must issue a VFD order before medicated feed can be obtained. Over-the-counter antibiotics previously available without veterinary oversight now require a prescription or VFD. This regulation supports antimicrobial stewardship and helps preserve antibiotic effectiveness.
